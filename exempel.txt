*Kopi luwak* is a coffee <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coffee> that
consists of partially digested coffee cherries
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coffee_cherry>, which have been eaten and
defecated <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defecation> by the Asian palm
civet <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asian_palm_civet> (/Paradoxurus
hermaphroditus/). It is therefore also called *civet coffee*. The
cherries are fermented <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermentation> as
they pass through a civet's intestines
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intestines>, and after being defecated
with other fecal matter, they are collected.^[1]
<#cite_note-Mahendradatta2012-1> Asian palm civets are increasingly
caught in the wild and traded for this purpose.^[2]
<#cite_note-Shepherd2012-2>

Kopi luwak is produced mainly on the Indonesian
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indonesia> islands of Sumatra
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumatra>, Java
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Java>, Bali
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bali>, Sulawesi
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulawesi>, and in East Timor
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Timor>. It is also widely gathered
in the forest or produced in farms in the islands of the Philippines
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippines>,^[3] <#cite_note-Ongo-3>
where the product is called /kape motit/ in the Cordillera region, /kapé
alamíd/ in Tagalog <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tagalog_language>
areas, /kapé melô/ or /kapé musang/ in Mindanao, and /kahawa kubing/ in
the Sulu Archipelago <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulu_Archipelago>.
/Weasel coffee/ is a loose English translation of its Vietnamese
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam> name /cà phê Chồn/.

Producers of the coffee beans argue that the process may improve coffee
through two mechanisms, selection – civets choosing to eat only certain
cherries – and digestion – biological or chemical mechanisms in the
animal's digestive tract altering the composition of the coffee cherries.

The traditional method of collecting feces from wild Asian palm civets
has given way to an intensive farming
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intensive_farming> method, in which the
palm civets are kept in battery cages
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battery_cage> and are force-fed the
cherries. This method of production has raised ethical concerns about
the treatment of civets and the conditions they are made to live in,
which include isolation, poor diet, small cages and a high mortality
rate.^[4] <#cite_note-Milman-4> ^[5] <#cite_note-5> ^[6]
<#cite_note-BBC20130913-6>

Although kopi luwak is a form of processing rather than a variety of
coffee, it has been called one of the most expensive coffees in the
world, with retail prices reaching €550 to US$700 per kilogram.^[7]
<#cite_note-7>

    History[edit
    <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kopi_luwak&action=edit&section=1>]

The origin of kopi luwak is closely connected to the history of coffee
production in Indonesia
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coffee_production_in_Indonesia>. Dutch
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_people> colonialists
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonialism> established coffee
plantations in Indonesia and imported beans from Yemen
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yemen>. In the 19th century, farmers in
central Java started to brew and drink coffee from excreted beans
collected in their plantations.^[8] <#cite_note-8>


    Production[edit
    <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kopi_luwak&action=edit&section=2>]

<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Luwak.jpg>
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Luwak.jpg>
An Asian palm civet
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Kopi_Luwak_already_digested_by_Asian_Palm_Civet.jpg>

<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Kopi_Luwak_already_digested_by_Asian_Palm_Civet.jpg>
Defecated luwak coffee berries in East Java
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Java>

Kopi luwak is brewed from coffee beans that transversed the
gastrointestinal tract
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract> of an Asian palm
civet, and were thus subjected to a combination of acidic, enzymatic
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enzymatic> and fermentation
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermentation> treatment. During
digestion, digestive enzymes
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digestive_enzyme> and gastric juices
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastric_juice> permeate through the
endocarp <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endocarp> of coffee cherries and
break down storage proteins
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Storage_protein>, yielding shorter
peptides <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peptide>. This alters the
composition of amino acids and impacts the aroma of the coffee. In the
roasting process, the proteins undergo a non-enzymatic Maillard reaction
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maillard_reaction>.^[9]
<#cite_note-marcone2-9> The palm civet is thought to select the most
ripe and flawless coffee cherries. This selection influences the flavour
of the coffee, as does the digestive process. The beans begin to
germinate <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germinate> by malting
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malt>, which reduces their
bitterness.^[10] <#cite_note-marcone1-10>

Traditionally, excreted coffee beans were collected directly in
plantations and forests. As the international demand for kopi luwak
increased, some producers turned to caged production methods to increase
yields. In 2014, the annual kopi luwak production was grossly estimated
at less than 127 kg. It is produced in Indonesia, East Timor
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Timor>, the Philippines
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Philippines>, Thailand
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thailand>, Vietnam
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam> and Ethiopia
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethiopia>.^[11] <#cite_note-11>


    Taste[edit
    <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kopi_luwak&action=edit&section=3>]

<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Kopi_Luwak_Gayo,_Takengon,_Aceh.jpg>
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Kopi_Luwak_Gayo,_Takengon,_Aceh.jpg>
A cup of kopi luwak from Gayo, Takengon, Aceh
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aceh>

The taste of kopi luwak varies with the type and origin of excreted
beans, processing, roasting, aging and brewing. The ability of the civet
to select its berries, and other aspects of the civet's diet and health,
like stress levels, may also influence the processing and hence
taste.^[12] <#cite_note-Onishi-12>

Within the coffee industry, kopi luwak is widely regarded as a gimmick
or novelty item. The Specialty Coffee Association of America
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specialty_Coffee_Association_of_America>
(SCAA) states that there is a "general consensus within the industry ...
it just tastes bad". A coffee professional compared the same beans with
and without the kopi luwak process using a rigorous coffee cupping
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coffee_cupping> evaluation. He concluded:
"it was apparent that luwak coffee sold for the story, not superior
quality... Using the SCAA cupping scale, the luwak scored two points
below the lowest of the other three coffees. It would appear that the
luwak processing diminishes good acidity and flavor and adds smoothness
to the body, which is what many people seem to note as a positive to the
coffee.”^[13] <#cite_note-Kubota-13> Professional coffee tasters were
able to distinguish kopi luwak from other coffee samples, but remarked
that it tasted "thin".^[14] <#cite_note-14> Some critics claim more
generally that kopi luwak is simply bad coffee, purchased for novelty
rather than taste.^[13] <#cite_note-Kubota-13> ^[15] <#cite_note-15>
^[16] <#cite_note-16> A food writer reviewed kopi luwak available to
American consumers and concluded "It tasted just like...Folgers
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Folgers>. Stale. Lifeless. Petrified
dinosaur droppings steeped in bathtub water. I couldn't finish it."^[17]
<#cite_note-17>


    Imitation[edit
    <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kopi_luwak&action=edit&section=4>]

Several commercial processes attempt to replicate the digestive process
of the civets without animal involvement. Researchers with the
University of Florida
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Florida> have been issued
with a patent for one such process.^[18] <#cite_note-18> ^[19]
<#cite_note-19> Brooklyn-based food startup Afineur has also developed a
patented fermentation technology that reproduces some of the taste
aspects of Kopi Luwak while improving coffee bean taste and nutritional
profile.^[20] <#cite_note-20> ^[21] <#cite_note-21> ^[22] <#cite_note-22>

Vietnamese companies sell an imitation kopi luwak, made using an enzyme
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enzyme> soak which they claim replicates
the civet's digestive process.^[23] <#cite_note-23>

Imitation has several motivations. The high price of kopi luwak drives
the search for a way to produce kopi luwak in large quantities. Kopi
luwak production involves a great deal of labour, whether farmed or
wild-gathered. The small production quantity and the labor involved in
production contribute to the coffee's high cost.^[24] <#cite_note-24>
Imitation may be a response to the decrease in the civet
population.^[25] <#cite_note-25>


    Animal welfare[edit
    <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kopi_luwak&action=edit&section=5>]

<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Luwak_(civet_cat)_in_cage.jpg>
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Luwak_(civet_cat)_in_cage.jpg>
Asian palm civet in a cage

Growing numbers of intensive civet "farms" have been established and are
operated in Southeast Asia, confining tens of thousands of animals to
live in battery cages <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battery_cage> and
be force-fed.^[26] <#cite_note-26> ^[27] <#cite_note-27> ^[28]
<#cite_note-28> "The conditions are awful, much like battery chickens",
said Chris Shepherd, deputy regional director of TRAFFIC
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traffic_(conservation_programme)> in
Southeast Asia. "The civets are taken from the wild and have to endure
horrific conditions. They fight to stay together but they are separated
and have to bear a very poor diet in very small cages. There is a high
mortality rate and for some species of civet, there's a real
conservation risk. It is spiralling out of control. But there's not much
public awareness of how it is actually made. People need to be aware
that tens of thousands of civets are being kept in these conditions. It
would put people off their coffee if they knew".^[4]
<#cite_note-Milman-4> The trade in palm civets for the production of
kopi luwak may constitute a significant threat to wild populations.^[2]
<#cite_note-Shepherd2012-2>

In 2013, People for the Ethical Treatment of Animals
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People_for_the_Ethical_Treatment_of_Animals>
(PETA) investigators found wild-caught civets on farms in Indonesia and
the Philippines. They were deprived of exercise, proper diet, and space.
Video footage from the investigation shows abnormal behaviours such as
repeated pacing, circling, or biting the bars of their cages. The
animals often lose their fur.^[29]
<#cite_note-Kopi_Luwak_Investigation-29> A BBC
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BBC> investigation revealed similar
conditions. Farmers using caged palm civets in north Sumatra confirmed
that they supplied kopi luwak beans to exporters whose produce ends up
in Europe and Asia.^[6] <#cite_note-BBC20130913-6> Tony Wild, the coffee
executive responsible for bringing kopi luwak to the Western world, has
stated he no longer supports using kopi luwak due to animal cruelty and
launched a campaign called "Cut the Crap" to halt the use of kopi
luwak.^[30] <#cite_note-30>


    Price and availability[edit
    <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kopi_luwak&action=edit&section=6>]

<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Luwak_Coffee_Display_In_An_Upscale_Coffee_Shop.jpg>

<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Luwak_Coffee_Display_In_An_Upscale_Coffee_Shop.jpg>
A window display in an upscale coffee shop showing Luwak Coffee in forms
of defecated clumps (bottom), unroasted beans (left) and roasted beans
(right)

Kopi luwak is one of the most expensive coffees in the world, selling
for between US$100 and $500 per pound in 2010.^[12]
<#cite_note-Onishi-12> The specialty Vietnamese /weasel coffee/, which
is made by collecting coffee beans eaten by wild civets, is sold at
US$500 per kilogram.^[31] <#cite_note-31> Most customers are Asian,
especially those originating from Japan, China and South Korea.^[32]
<#cite_note-BBC-32> Sources vary widely as to annual worldwide production.

The price of farmed (considered low-grade by connoisseurs) kopi luwak in
large Indonesian supermarkets is from US$100 per kilogram (five times
the price of a high quality local arabica coffee).

The price paid to collectors in the Philippines is closer to US$20 per
kilogram.^[12] <#cite_note-Onishi-12>

Some specialty coffee shops sell cups of brewed kopi luwak for
US$35–80.^[33] <#cite_note-33> ^[34] <#cite_note-34> ^[35] <#cite_note-35>


    Authenticity and fraud[edit
    <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kopi_luwak&action=edit&section=7>]

Investigations by PETA
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People_for_the_Ethical_Treatment_of_Animals>
and the BBC found fraud to be rife in the kopi luwak industry, with
producers willing to label coffee from caged civets with a "wild
sourced" or similar label.^[29] <#cite_note-Kopi_Luwak_Investigation-29>
^[6] <#cite_note-BBC20130913-6>

Genuine kopi luwak from wild civets is difficult to purchase in
Indonesia and proving it is not fake is very difficult – there is little
enforcement regarding use of the name "kopi luwak", and there's even a
local cheap coffee brand named "Luwak", which costs under US$3 per
kilogram but is occasionally sold online under the guise of real kopi
luwak.


    Variations[edit
    <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kopi_luwak&action=edit&section=8>]

There are reports of a kopi luwak type process occurring naturally with
muntjac <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muntjac> and birds. Bat coffee is
another variation that is in demand. Bats feed on the ripest coffee and
fruits and spit out the seeds. These seeds are dried and processed to
make coffee with a slight fruity flavor.^[36] <#cite_note-36> ^[37]
<#cite_note-37>


    In culture[edit
    <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kopi_luwak&action=edit&section=9>]

In the movie /The Bucket List
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Bucket_List>/, billionaire health
care magnate Edward Cole enjoys drinking kopi luwak, but is unaware of
how it is produced. Carter Chambers explains how civets defecate kopi
luwak coffee beans and that its gastric juices give the unique aroma to
this coffee.

The Japanese manga series /Beastars
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beastars>/ features an anthropomorphic
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthropomorphism> civet character that
produces kopi luwak.^[38] <#cite_note-38>
